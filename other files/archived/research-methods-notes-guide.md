## PART 0: NON-NEGOTIABLE FORMATTING RULES (THE "FAIL-SAFE CHECKLIST")

### A. PAGE SETUP (Microsoft Word Settings)

**Margins:**
- Left Margin: **1.5 inches** (this is the binding side)
- Right Margin: **1.0 inch**
- Top Margin: **1.0 inch**
- Bottom Margin: **1.0 inch**

**How to Set in Word:** Page Layout → Margins → Custom Margins → Enter values exactly as above.

**Font and Size:**
- Font Family: **Times New Roman**
- Font Size: **12 points**
- Font Color: **Black** (Automatic/Black, no gray, no custom colors)

**Line Spacing:**
- **Double spacing** throughout the ENTIRE manuscript
- **No exceptions** (not 1.5, not 2.5, exactly 2.0)
- How to Set: Home → Line Spacing → 2.0

**Paragraph Formatting:**
- First Line Indentation: **5 spaces** (or 0.5 inch tab stop)
- Alignment: **Justified** (both left and right edges aligned)
- **CRITICAL:** Do NOT use bullets or numbering in Scope and Limitation, Software Model discussion, Research Instruments, or Data Analysis sections.

---

### B. PAGE NUMBERING RULES (ULTRA-SPECIFIC PLACEMENT)

**For Main Chapter Pages (Chapter 1, 2, 3, etc.):**
- Location: **Top-Right corner**
- Format: Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3, 4...)
- Special Rule: Page 1 (Title Page) must have numbering **invisible/hidden**
- Visibility starts: Page 2 onwards (the page after Title Page)
- How to Do This in Word:
  1. Insert → Page Number → Top of Page → Plain Number 3 (right-aligned)
  2. Double-click header area on Page 1
  3. Check "Different First Page" in Header & Footer Tools
  4. The number will disappear from Page 1 but remain on all subsequent pages

**For Appendices Pages:**
- Location: **Bottom-Center**
- Format: **Roman Numerals** (i, ii, iii, iv, v...)
- How to Do This in Word:
  1. Place cursor on first Appendix page
  2. Page Layout → Breaks → Next Page (to create a new section)
  3. Insert → Page Number → Bottom of Page → Plain Number 2 (centered)
  4. Click "Format Page Numbers"
  5. Change Number Format to "i, ii, iii..."
  6. Start at: i

---

### C. TABLE FORMATTING RULES (THE "3-LINE OPEN TABLE" STANDARD)

**Visual Structure:**
- Tables must have **ONLY 3 visible lines:**
  1. Top border (above header row)
  2. Middle border (below header row, separating header from data)
  3. Bottom border (below last data row)
- **NO side borders** (left and right sides must be open)
- **NO internal vertical lines** between columns

**Border Specifications:**
- Thickness: **1.5 point**
- Color: Black

**How to Create This in Word:**
1. Insert your table (Insert → Table)
2. Select the entire table
3. Right-click → Borders and Shading
4. Click "None" to remove all borders
5. Click the **Top** border button (preview shows line at top)
6. Click the **Bottom** border button (preview shows line at bottom)
7. Select ONLY the header row (first row)
8. Right-click → Borders and Shading
9. Click the **Bottom** border button (this creates the middle line under headers)
10. In all steps, ensure Line Width is set to **1½ pt**

**Table Placement Rules:**
- Tables **CANNOT** be split across two pages
- If a table is too long, you must either:
  - Adjust row height/font size to fit on one page, OR
  - Manually split it and use "Table X (Continued)" on the second page
- Preferred: Keep tables on one page whenever possible

**Standard Table Title Format:**
```
Table 1. Respondents

Respondents          n
Manager              1
Owner                1
Customers           50
IT Experts           5
```

**Column Header "n":**
- When showing sample size, use lowercase **"n"** (not N, not No., not Number)
- This is APA standard notation for sample size

### 1. BACKGROUND OF THE STUDY (BoS)

This is the most literature-heavy section. It follows a strict funnel structure.

#### **Paragraph 1: The Opening Hook (NO CITATIONS ALLOWED)**

- Length: 4-6 sentences
- Content: Write in YOUR OWN WORDS based on your observation or general knowledge
- Purpose: Introduce the general problem area without citing anyone
- **ABSOLUTE RULE:** NO literature, NO citations, NO references in this paragraph
- Example approach: "In modern educational institutions, scheduling challenges have become increasingly complex. Multiple stakeholders compete for limited resources while maintaining academic quality. Traditional manual scheduling methods often result in conflicts and inefficiencies. These challenges are magnified in institutions with growing student populations and limited physical infrastructure."

#### **The Funnel Structure (Global → National → Local)**

You MUST have exactly **15 sources**:
- **5 Global sources** (international studies, worldwide trends)
- **5 National sources** (Philippine context, national studies)
- **5 Local sources** (regional studies, local government data, local institutional research)

**Publication Year Requirement:**
- ALL 15 sources must be published between **2021–2026**
- No exceptions (not 2020, not 2019, even if the study is excellent)

#### **Next Paragraph/s : Global Scenario (5 sources)**

- Length: 8-12 sentences
- Start broad: Discuss the problem from a worldwide perspective
- Cite international journals, studies from other countries, WHO/UN reports (if applicable)
- Each source should contribute 1-2 sentences of discussion
- **Integration is key:** Don't just list "Author1 said this. Author2 said that." Instead, weave them together thematically.
- Example structure:
  - Sentence 1-2: Global prevalence of the problem (Source 1)
  - Sentence 3-4: Impact in developed countries (Source 2)
  - Sentence 5-6: Emerging trends globally (Source 3)
  - Sentence 7-8: Technological responses worldwide (Source 4)
  - Sentence 9-10: Challenges identified internationally (Source 5)

#### **Next Paragraph/s: National Scenario (5 sources)**

- Length: 8-12 sentences
- Narrow down: Discuss the problem in the **Philippine context**
- Cite Philippine journals, CHED memorandums, DepEd orders, Filipino researchers, local news analyses
- Connect to Filipino culture, educational system, or local policies where relevant
- Example structure:
  - Sentence 1-2: The problem in Philippine education (Source 6)
  - Sentence 3-4: Government initiatives or policies (Source 7)
  - Sentence 5-6: Challenges specific to Philippine institutions (Source 8)
  - Sentence 7-8: Filipino researchers' findings (Source 9)
  - Sentence 9-10: Current state in Philippine schools/universities (Source 10)

#### **Next Paragraph/s: Local Scenario (5 sources)**

- Length: 8-12 sentences
- Most specific: Discuss the problem in YOUR REGION or YOUR INSTITUTION
- Cite local university research, regional studies, institutional data, local government reports
- This is where you connect to your ACTUAL research site
- Example structure:
  - Sentence 1-2: The problem in your region/institution (Source 11)
  - Sentence 3-4: Local attempts to address it (Source 12)
  - Sentence 5-6: Specific challenges in your locale (Source 13)
  - Sentence 7-8: Local stakeholder perspectives (Source 14)
  - Sentence 9-10: Current gap in your specific context (Source 15)

#### **Next Paragraph: Synthesis/Summary of Literature**

- Length: 5-7 sentences
- Purpose: Tie together the Global, National, and Local discussions
- Show the common threads across all 15 sources
- Identify the research gap that emerges from the literature
- **Do NOT introduce new citations here** – synthesize what you already cited
- Example: "The literature reveals a consistent pattern across global, national, and local contexts: scheduling inefficiencies persist despite technological advances. While international studies highlight algorithmic solutions, Philippine research emphasizes the need for context-appropriate systems. Local investigations confirm that existing tools fail to address institution-specific constraints. This gap between available technology and actual implementation needs provides the foundation for the present study."

#### **Last Paragraph: The Closing (Clinching Statement + Justification)**

- Length: 5-7 sentences
- **Part A – Clinching Statement:** Explicitly state HOW the literature assisted you
  - Example: "The reviewed literature provided critical insights into scheduling complexities, stakeholder needs, and technological approaches. These studies informed the researchers' understanding of essential system features and potential implementation challenges."
- **Part B – Justification:** Explain WHY you selected this topic
  - Example: "The researchers selected this topic due to observable scheduling conflicts at [Your Institution], which result in classroom shortages and professor overloading. Addressing this problem would benefit students, faculty, and administrators."
- **Part C – Importance Statement:** Emphasize the significance
  - Example: "Efficient scheduling systems are essential for academic quality and resource optimization, making this research both timely and necessary."
- **Part D – Final Statement:** Connect directly to your research problem
  - Example: "Given the identified gap between existing scheduling practices and institutional needs, the development of a context-specific scheduling system becomes imperative."

### 2. CONCEPTUAL FRAMEWORK

This section requires both a VISUAL diagram and a DISCUSSION.

#### **Model Selection (Two Options)**

**Option A: Input-Process-Output (IPO) Model**
- This is the STANDARD and most commonly accepted
- Your instructor's template shows this as the preferred model
- Safe choice for defense

**Option B: System Architecture Models**
- Examples: V-Model, Spiral Model, etc.
- Your instructor allows this per the user's note
- Use ONLY if you have a strong justification
- Riskier for defense (panel may ask why you didn't use IPO)

#### **If Using IPO Model (Recommended):**

**Visual Requirements:**
- Create a diagram with three boxes connected by arrows
- Box 1 (Left): **INPUT**
- Arrow pointing right
- Box 2 (Center): **PROCESS**
- Arrow pointing right
- Box 3 (Right): **OUTPUT**

**Content of Each Box:**

**INPUT Box:**
- List the specific VARIABLES that will be fed into your system
- These are NOT general concepts – they must be CONCRETE data elements
- **Example for a Room Scheduling System:**
  - Rooms (Room Numbers, Capacity, Equipment)
  - Subjects (Subject Codes, Units, Type)
  - Professors (Names, Teaching Loads, Specializations)
  - Schedules (Time Slots, Days, Academic Calendar)
  - Policies (No-conflict rules, Maximum hours per day)
- **DO NOT write vague items** like "User Requirements" or "Data" – be SPECIFIC

**PROCESS Box:**
- Write the EXACT NAME of your system
- **Example:** "Visual Room Scheduling System"
- **Example:** "E-TrikeHub: Online Tricycle Booking Platform"
- This should match your title exactly

**OUTPUT Box:**
- List the TANGIBLE RESULTS or REPORTS your system will generate
- These must be SPECIFIC deliverables
- **Example for a Room Scheduling System:**
  - Professor Teaching Load Reports
  - Room Utilization Schedules
  - Conflict-Free Timetables
  - Room Availability Dashboard
  - Weekly Schedule Printouts
- **DO NOT write** "Solutions" or "Results" – be CONCRETE

**Figure Labeling:**
- Below the diagram, center-aligned:
- `Figure 1. Conceptual Framework of the Study`

#### **Discussion of the Framework (MANDATORY)**

You CANNOT just insert the diagram and move on. You MUST discuss it.

**Discussion Structure (Paragraph Form, 6-8 sentences):**

1. **Opening:** Name the framework you're using
   - Example: "The study employs the Input-Process-Output (IPO) Model as its conceptual framework."

2. **Discuss INPUT:** Explain what goes into the system and why these specific inputs were selected
   - Example: "The input component consists of essential scheduling variables including room data (capacity, equipment, availability), subject information (codes, units, type), professor details (names, loads, specializations), time slot parameters, and institutional scheduling policies. These inputs represent the fundamental data elements required to generate optimized schedules."

3. **Discuss PROCESS:** Explain what the system DOES with the inputs
   - Example: "The process component represents the Visual Room Scheduling System itself, which algorithmically analyzes the input data, applies constraint-solving logic based on institutional policies, and generates conflict-free schedule combinations. The system automates what was previously a manual, time-intensive process."

4. **Discuss OUTPUT:** Explain what the system produces and how it solves the problem
   - Example: "The output component delivers tangible scheduling solutions including professor teaching load reports, room utilization schedules, and conflict-free timetables. These outputs enable administrators to visualize resource allocation and make data-driven scheduling decisions."

5. **Explain the CONNECTIONS:** This is critical – show how the three boxes relate
   - Example: "The framework demonstrates a logical flow: input data feeds into the processing system, which transforms raw information into actionable scheduling outputs. Each component is interdependent; incomplete inputs produce unreliable outputs, while effective processing ensures optimal resource utilization."

6. **Closing:** Relate the framework back to your research objectives
   - Example: "This framework aligns with the study's objectives by clearly delineating the system's scope, processing methodology, and expected deliverables."

### 3. OBJECTIVES OF THE STUDY

This section has a strict two-part structure: ONE General Objective and THREE Specific Objectives.

#### **General Objective (Single Statement)**

**Format:**
- Starts with: "The general objective of this project is to develop..."
- Must include:
  1. System name (with branding if applicable)
  2. Brief purpose statement
  3. Key benefits or goals

**Example:**
"The general objective of this project is to develop E-TrikeHub: A Smart and Hassle-Free Online Tricycle Booking Platform, which aims to provide a seamless, efficient, and user-friendly digital solution for booking tricycle rides. This system will enhance local transportation by optimizing ride scheduling, improving commuter convenience, and supporting tricycle operators through a secure and automated platform."

**Template You Can Follow:**
"The general objective of this project is to develop [System Name with Branding], which aims to [primary purpose]. This system will [benefit 1], [benefit 2], and [benefit 3]."

#### **Specific Objectives (Numbered List 1-3)**

**Opening Line:**
"Specifically, this study aimed:"

**The Three Objectives Follow a Standard Pattern:**

**Objective 1: ASSESSMENT/INPUT**
- Purpose: Identify what currently exists or what inputs are needed
- Keyword: "To identify..." or "To determine..."
- **Example 1:** "To identify the existing processes and policy of tricycle booking;"
- **Example 2:** "To determine the current room scheduling procedures and constraints at [Institution Name];"
- Focus: Current state, existing systems, policies, or required inputs

**Objective 2: DEVELOPMENT**
- Purpose: Identify what features or requirements the system needs
- Keyword: "To determine..." or "To identify..."
- **Example 1:** "To determine the functional and non-functional requirements;"
- **Example 2:** "To identify the system features and user requirements for the scheduling platform;"
- Focus: System specifications, technical requirements, features

**Objective 3: EVALUATION**
- Purpose: Assess how well the system works
- Keyword: "To evaluate..." or "To assess..."
- Must specify the evaluation instrument
- **Example 1:** "To evaluate the usability of the proposed system using SUS questionnaire."
- **Example 2:** "To assess the level of acceptability of the system based on ISO 9126 quality standards."
- Focus: System testing, user acceptance, quality metrics

**Formatting Notes:**
- Each objective ends with a semicolon (;) EXCEPT the last one, which ends with a period (.)
- Each objective is numbered (1., 2., 3.)
- Each objective starts with "To [verb]..."
- Objectives must be parallel in structure

### 4. SCOPE AND LIMITATION OF THE STUDY

**CRITICAL FORMAT RULE:** This MUST be written in **paragraph form ONLY**. NO bullets, NO numbering, NO lists.

#### **Structure: Two Separate Paragraphs**

**Paragraph 1: SCOPE (What IS Included)**

Length: 6-10 sentences

Must discuss the following elements:

1. **Authorized Users/User Types:**
   - Who can access the system and their roles
   - Example: "The system accommodates three user types: administrators, faculty members, and students. Administrators have full access to scheduling configuration and report generation. Faculty members can view their assigned schedules and submit availability constraints. Students can access the published class timetables."

2. **Features Included:**
   - What functionalities the system provides
   - Be specific about modules or capabilities
   - Example: "The system includes modules for room allocation, conflict detection, schedule visualization, and automated report generation. Users can filter schedules by room, professor, or time slot."

3. **Principal Variables:**
   - What data elements the system handles
   - Example: "The system processes variables including room capacity, equipment requirements, subject types, time blocks, and professor preferences."

4. **Locale (Geographic/Institutional Scope):**
   - Where the study is conducted and where the system will be implemented
   - Example: "The study is conducted at [Specific Institution Name], [City/Municipality], [Province], Philippines. The system is designed specifically for this institution's scheduling needs."

5. **Timeframe:**
   - When the study/development occurred
   - Example: "The research and development process spans from June 2025 to February 2026, covering one full academic year cycle."

6. **Justification:**
   - Why these boundaries were chosen
   - Example: "This scope ensures the system addresses the institution's immediate scheduling challenges while remaining manageable within the capstone project timeline."

**Example Complete Scope Paragraph:**
"The study focuses on developing a room scheduling system for [Institution Name], located in [City], [Province], Philippines, during the period of June 2025 to February 2026. The system is designed to serve three user types: administrators who manage scheduling configurations and generate reports, faculty members who view assigned schedules and submit availability preferences, and students who access published class timetables. The system includes core modules for room allocation, automated conflict detection, visual schedule representation, and comprehensive report generation with filtering capabilities by room, professor, or time slot. Principal variables processed by the system include room numbers and capacities, equipment availability, subject codes and types, time block configurations, academic calendar dates, and professor teaching preferences. This scope was chosen to address the institution's most pressing scheduling inefficiencies while ensuring the project remains achievable within the academic year timeframe and available resources."

**Paragraph 2: LIMITATION (What IS NOT Included)**

Length: 5-8 sentences

Must discuss:

1. **Excluded Features/Functionalities:**
   - What the system does NOT do
   - Example: "The system does not include automated grading integration, student enrollment processing, or financial accounting modules."

2. **Boundaries/Constraints:**
   - Technical or practical limitations
   - Example: "The system is limited to desktop browser access and does not include a mobile application. Real-time synchronization across multiple campuses is excluded from this version."

3. **Excluded User Groups:**
   - Who cannot access the system
   - Example: "The system does not accommodate external stakeholders such as parents or alumni."

4. **Data Limitations:**
   - What data is not processed or stored
   - Example: "The system does not store historical scheduling data beyond the current academic year. Student academic records and personal information are not included in the database."

5. **Geographic/Institutional Limitations:**
   - Where the system will NOT be implemented
   - Example: "The system is designed exclusively for [Your Institution] and has not been tested for compatibility with other educational institutions' scheduling policies."

**Example Complete Limitation Paragraph:**
"The study excludes several functionalities to maintain focus on core scheduling features. The system does not include student enrollment management, grading systems, financial modules, or attendance tracking. Access is limited to desktop web browsers; mobile application development and offline functionality are not part of this version. The system does not accommodate external stakeholders such as parents, alumni, or external auditors. Data storage is confined to the current and upcoming academic year; historical archival beyond this period is not implemented. Real-time multi-campus synchronization is excluded, as the system is designed specifically for [Institution Name]'s single-campus environment. These limitations allow the researchers to deliver a focused, functional scheduling solution within the capstone project's scope and timeline."

### 1. RESEARCH DESIGN

This section requires you to define, cite, and apply two types of research design.

#### **The Required Design: Descriptive Developmental Research Design**

**Structure (3-4 Paragraphs):**

**Paragraph 1: Define "Descriptive Research"**

- Length: 3-4 sentences
- Must include a citation (recommended: Siedlecki, 2020, or any research methods textbook from 2021-2026)
- Example:
  "Descriptive research design aims to accurately and systematically describe a population, situation, or phenomenon (Siedlecki, 2020). It answers questions about what exists, what is happening, or what the current state of a subject is, without manipulating variables. This design allows researchers to collect data that describes characteristics, behaviors, or conditions as they naturally occur."

**Paragraph 2: Define "Developmental Research"**

- Length: 3-4 sentences
- Must include a citation (find a source that discusses developmental research in technology/education)
- Example:
  "Developmental research focuses on the creation, development, and validation of products, processes, or systems (Author, Year). In the context of information technology, developmental research involves designing and building functional prototypes or systems that address identified needs. This approach emphasizes iterative development, testing, and refinement based on user feedback."

**Paragraph 3: Application to YOUR Study**

- Length: 4-6 sentences
- Explain how DESCRIPTIVE design applies to your work
- Explain how DEVELOPMENTAL design applies to your work
- Example:
  "The descriptive component of this study involves systematically identifying and documenting the current scheduling processes, constraints, and requirements at [Institution Name]. Through surveys and interviews with administrators, faculty, and students, the researchers gather data that describes existing practices, challenges, and user needs. The developmental component encompasses the actual design, creation, and iterative refinement of the Visual Room Scheduling System. This involves translating identified requirements into functional features, building working prototypes, and conducting usability evaluations. Together, these approaches ensure that the system is grounded in empirical understanding of user needs while following rigorous development practices."

**Paragraph 4 (Optional): Justification**

- Length: 2-3 sentences
- Explain why this design is appropriate
- Example:
  "This combined descriptive-developmental approach is appropriate because it allows the researchers to both understand the problem context and create a practical solution. The descriptive foundation ensures the system addresses real needs, while the developmental process delivers a functional product."


#### **The Discussion (MUST BE PARAGRAPH FORM)**

**CRITICAL RULE:** DO NOT use bullets or numbered lists. Write in flowing paragraphs.

**Structure (2-3 Paragraphs):**

**Paragraph 1: Introduction to the Model**

- Length: 3-4 sentences
- Name the model
- Cite the source (author and year)
- Briefly describe its purpose or philosophy
- Example:
  "The study employs the Rapid Application Development (RAD) Model as outlined by Kendall and Kendall (2021). RAD is an iterative software development methodology that emphasizes rapid prototyping and quick feedback over lengthy development and testing cycles. This model is particularly suited for projects with well-defined business objectives and tight timeframes, as it allows for concurrent development activities and frequent stakeholder involvement."

**Paragraph 2: Discussion of the Phases**

- Length: 8-12 sentences
- Discuss EACH phase of the model in order
- Explain what happens in each phase
- NO bullets, NO numbered lists – write in continuous prose

**Example for RAD Model:**
"The RAD model consists of four primary phases that guide the development process. The first phase, Requirements Planning, involves intensive collaboration between developers and users to define system objectives, identify information needs, and establish project scope. During this phase, the researchers conducted stakeholder interviews and gathered functional requirements from administrators, faculty, and students at [Institution Name]. The second phase, User Design, focuses on rapid prototyping and iterative design refinement. Users are actively involved in reviewing prototypes, providing feedback, and shaping the system's interface and functionality. The researchers developed initial interface mockups and scheduling algorithm prototypes, which were presented to users for validation and modification. The third phase, Construction, involves the actual coding and integration of system components. Developers transform approved prototypes into working software, implement database structures, and integrate various modules. The researchers utilized [programming languages/frameworks] to build the scheduling engine, user interface, and reporting modules. The final phase, Cutover, encompasses testing, user training, data conversion, and system deployment. The researchers conducted usability testing with representative users, gathered feedback using the System Usability Scale (SUS), and prepared documentation and training materials. Throughout all phases, the RAD model's emphasis on iteration and user involvement ensured that the system continuously evolved to meet actual user needs."

**Paragraph 3 (Optional): Justification**

- Length: 2-3 sentences
- Explain why this model is appropriate for your project
- Example:
  "The RAD model is appropriate for this capstone project because it accommodates the academic timeline constraints while ensuring meaningful user involvement. The iterative nature allows for flexibility in addressing unforeseen challenges during development."

## PART III: WRITING STYLE AND LANGUAGE GUIDELINES

### A. CITATION AND REFERENCING (APA 7th Edition)

#### **In-Text Citations:**

**Format for paraphrasing:**
- Single author: (Smith, 2023)
- Two authors: (Smith & Jones, 2023)
- Three or more authors: (Smith et al., 2023)

**Format for direct quotes:**
- Short quotes (less than 40 words): "Text of quote" (Author, Year, p. 24).
- Long quotes (40+ words): Use block quote format (indent 0.5", no quotation marks)

**Examples:**
- "Recent studies demonstrate that automated scheduling systems reduce conflicts by up to 60% (Garcia, 2024)."
- "According to Martinez and Lopez (2023), educational institutions increasingly adopt technology-driven scheduling solutions."
- "Multiple researchers have identified scheduling inefficiencies as a critical administrative burden (Chen et al., 2022)."

#### **Reference List Format (At the end of your manuscript):**

**Journal Article:**
```
Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article. Title of Periodical, volume(issue), pages. https://doi.org/xxxxx
```

**Book:**
```
Author, A. A. (Year). Title of book. Publisher.
```

**Website:**
```
Author, A. A. (Year, Month Day). Title of page. Website Name. URL
```

---

### B. ACADEMIC WRITING TONE

**DO:**
- Write in third person ("The researchers developed..." NOT "We developed...")
- Use past tense for completed actions ("The system was developed...")
- Use present tense for current facts ("The system includes...")
- Maintain formal, objective language
- Be specific and precise

**DO NOT:**
- Use first person (I, we, our, us) in the manuscript
- Use contractions (don't → do not, can't → cannot)
- Use colloquial language or slang
- Use emotional or subjective language
- Use vague terms (very, a lot, many) – be specific with numbers

**Examples:**

❌ WRONG: "We think our system is really good and will help a lot of people."
✅ CORRECT: "The developed system demonstrates significant potential to improve scheduling efficiency for administrators, faculty, and students."

❌ WRONG: "The researchers found out that scheduling is super difficult."
✅ CORRECT: "The researchers identified scheduling complexity as a significant administrative challenge, characterized by multiple conflicting constraints and limited automation."

---

### C. PARAGRAPH CONSTRUCTION

**Each Paragraph Should:**
- Have a clear topic sentence (first sentence introduces the main idea)
- Include supporting sentences with evidence/examples
- End with a concluding or transitional sentence
- Be 5-10 sentences long (avoid very short 2-sentence paragraphs)
- Maintain coherence (all sentences relate to the topic sentence)

**Transition Words to Connect Ideas:**
- To add information: Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally, In addition
- To show contrast: However, Nevertheless, Conversely, In contrast
- To show cause/effect: Therefore, Consequently, As a result, Thus
- To give examples: For instance, For example, Specifically, Particularly
- To sequence: First, Second, Subsequently, Finally

---

### D. COMMON GRAMMATICAL ERRORS TO AVOID

1. **Subject-Verb Agreement:**
   - ❌ "The system allow users to..."
   - ✅ "The system allows users to..."

2. **Article Usage (a/an/the):**
   - ❌ "System was developed..."
   - ✅ "The system was developed..."

3. **Comma Splices:**
   - ❌ "The study used RAD model, it has four phases."
   - ✅ "The study used the RAD model, which has four phases."

4. **Sentence Fragments:**
   - ❌ "Because the system reduces conflicts."
   - ✅ "The system is valuable because it reduces conflicts."
